## SCYLLA - Complete Mythological Analysis

### ORIGINAL SOURCE QUOTES

**HOMER - Odyssey XII (c. 8th century BCE)**
Original Greek: "ἔνθα δὲ Σκύλλη δοιαὶ ἔριδι πτολεμίζετο φωτὸς | ἓξ κεφαλαί..."

**Translation (Shewring):**
"Halfway up the cliff is a murky cave, facing Erebos, and doubtless it is past this, Odysseus, that you and your men will steer your vessel... Inside lives Skylla, yelping hideously; her voice is no deeper than a young puppy's but she herself is a fearsome monster... She has twelve feet all dangling down, six long necks with a grisly head on each of them, and in each head a triple row of crowded and close-set teeth, fraught with black death."

**Critical detail from Circe's warning:**
"Keep closer to Skylla's cliff, and row hard and invoke Krataiis; she is Skylla's mother; it is she who bore her to plague mankind."

**OVID - Metamorphoses XIV (c. 8 CE)**
Original Latin: "in vada praeceps"
Translation: "She has a girl's sweet face... she was a sweet girl once."

Ovid's Circe transformation account (13.732ff):
"Circe, envious of Scylla's beauty, poisoned the well where Scylla bathed. When Scylla stepped into the water, dogs sprouted from her thighs."

**VIRGIL - Aeneid III (19 BCE)**
"Her upper part is human--a girl's beautiful body down to the privates; below, she is a weird sea-monster, with dolphin's tail and a belly of wolverine sort."

### EVOLUTION OF SCYLLA DEPIECTION

| Author | Physical Form | Key Innovation |
|--------|--------------|---------------|
| Homer | "twelve feet, six necks, three rows of teeth" - NO mention of dogs | Original "monster from birth" |
| Apollodorus | "face/breast of woman, six dog-heads, twelve dog-feet" | Added canine elements |
| Ovid | "beautiful nymph transformed by Circe" | Origin story added |
| Virgil | "human down to privates, dolphin's tail, wolverine belly" | Artistic interpretation |
| Suda (Byzantine) | "beautiful woman to eyes, six dog heads each side, serpent body" | Final composite form |

### KEY FACTS
- Scylla literally means "tear, rend" in Greek (skullō)
- Her name is etymologically related to "hermit-crab" (skyllaros), "dog" (skyllax), and "dog-shark"
- The Idiom "between Scylla and Charybdis" predates "between a rock and a hard place"
- Six heads = six product lines or six market segments
- Homer says her voice was "no deeper than a puppy" - ironically cute for such a monster

### BRANDING QUOTES
"Better by far to lose six men and keep your ship than lose your entire crew."
"No seaman ever, in any vessel, has boasted of sailing that way unharmed."
"She has a girl's sweet face... she was a sweet girl once."

## PLINY THE ELDER - Giant Cephalopods

### ORIGINAL LATIN TEXT
From Natural History Book IX (Marine Animals):

**Latin (Mayhoff edition):**
"Polypus vastus, qualis in Italia capitur, branchiis e complexu spherae adsimulatis, media in testa concamerata, quae pelagi pars maximae similis esse glomus dicitur..."

**Translation (Bostock & Riley):**
"The larger specimens of the polypus are found on the coast of Italy; they have respiratory organs, and a body of a spherical form, with the shell in the middle, which is very like the great part of the sea; and the whole is termed a 'glomus'."

### THE FAMOUS GIANT CEPHALOPOD ACCOUNT
Pliny describes a creature that attacked wrecked sailors:
"Polypus ingens... who had seized upon the men as they were swimming; he threw his arms around them, and prevented them from escaping; upon which the monster was killed by the swimmers."

### KEY FACTS
- Pliny died observing the eruption of Vesuvius (79 CE) - the ultimate field researcher
- His nephew published his work posthumously
- Pliny claimed 20,000 facts from 2,000 books
- Natural History was the largest surviving Roman work until modern encyclopedias
- He described 500+ animal species accurately

## ARISTOTLE - Cephalopod Intelligence

### WHAT ARISTOTLE GOT RIGHT (Historia Animalium, Book IV)

**ACCURATE OBSERVATIONS:**
1. **Tentacle structure**: "The octopus has eight tentacles... arranged around the mouth"
2. **Locomotion**: "It moves by means of its arms, and also employs a kind of flying leap"
3. **Color change**: "The octopus can change color to match its surroundings"
4. **Ink defense**: Described ink discharge accurately
5. **Feeding**: "It seizes prey with its arms and holds it until paralyzed"
6. **Intelligence**: Noted problem-solving abilities
7. **Reproduction**: Described egg-laying accurately

**Translation (D'Arcy Thompson):**
"The octopus has a coat of many colors... it changes to match the rock it is resting upon."

### THE ARGONAUT CONNECTION
Aristotle originated the myth that argonauts use two arms as sails:
"The argonaut spreads two of its arms to the wind and is carried along."

**FUN FACT**: This was believed for 2,000 years until Jeanne Villepreux-Power disproved it in 1830s.

## THE KRAKEN - Complete Historical Account

### Etymology
From Norwegian "kraken" - definite form of "krake" meaning "crooked creature" or "crookie" (from Old Norse "kraki" = hook)

### Christen Jensøn (1646)
First written description - sea monster with many arms that grabs boats and pulls them down into the abyss

### Olaus Magnus (1539-1555)
Carta marina showed sea monster between Norway and Iceland with tentacles growing from head. His 1555 *Historia de Gentibus Septentrionalibus* described:
- Black coloring
- Head 10-12 cubits long (~6-7 meters)
- Body 14-15 cubits (~8-9 meters)
- Eyes with 16-20 foot circumference, flaming red pupils
- "Beard" of hairs thick as goose feathers

### Erik Pontoppidan (1753)
Most influential description:
- "The monster is round, flat, and full of arms... the largest and most surprising of all the animal creation"
- Size: "quarter of a mile in circumference" when at surface
- Creates whirlpool/suction vortex when diving
- "If its arms laid hold of the largest man-of-war, they would pull it down to the bottom"

### Hans Egede (1729)
Described kraken's body as "many miles in length" with "many heads and a number of claws"

### Francesco Negri (1700)
Italian writer described *"sciu-crak"* as a massive "fish" with many horns or many arms, distinguished from sea serpents.

### Søren Richart Hagerup (ca. 1770)
Anonymous glossary described the kraken as a giant fish having tree-like fins, able to sink a whole boat.

### Famous Tennyson Poem - "The Kraken" (1830):
"Below the thunders of the upper deep,
Far, far beneath in the abysmal sea,
His ancient, dreamless, uninvaded sleep
The Kraken sleepeth..."

### Fishermen's Sayings
- "You must have fished on Kraken" - when catch was bountiful
- Pronounce its name to make it descend
- fishermen took the risk because the catch was so plentiful

## HAFGUFA - Medieval Sea Monster

### Etymology
Old Norse "haf" (sea) + "gufa" (steam/vapor) = "sea-reek"

### Source: King's Mirror (Konungs skuggsjá, c. 1250)
"Most often in our tongue we call it *hafgufa*."
"There is a fish that is still unmentioned, which it is scarcely advisable to speak about on account of its size, because it will seem to most people incredible..."

**Key Description:**
- Appeared like land more than fish when surfacing
- Only two exist (believed infertile)
- Opens mouth like "a great sound or fjord" - fish swim in, then it closes jaws
- The King himself feared it but doubted anyone would believe his account

### Örvar-Odds saga (14th century)
Describes ship sailing BETWEEN the creature's jaws - the "rocks" were its nostrils and lower jaw

### 2023 Scientific Discovery
Whale "trap-feeding" behavior matches hafgufa description exactly - whales stay stationary with jaws open, appearing like islands, attracting fish

## SEA MONK (1546)

**Discovery:** Creature captured in Øresund strait (Denmark/Sweden) described as a "fish" resembling a human monk, 4 ells long (~15 feet). Sent to Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor.

**Sources:**
- Pierre Belon (1553)
- Guillaume Rondelet (1554)
- Conrad Gesner (1558)
- German rhyme about it

**Japetus Steenstrup (1850s):** Suggested it was a giant squid - tentacles resembling monk's robes

**Modern theories:** Angelshark? Hoax? Jenny haniver (carved skate fins)?

---

## PIERRE DENYS DE MONTFORT (1801)

French malacologist who published famous engravings of "colossal octopus" attacking ships.

**The famous engraving:** Shows ship being attacked by many-armed creature with tentacles climbing the mast

**The British warships incident:** Montfort claimed 10 British warships disappeared in 1782 attacked by giant octopuses. The British historical record showed what actually happened - destroyed his credibility.

**His downfall:** Died "starving and poor in Paris around 1820"

**Legacy:** Influenced Victor Hugo's *Toilers of the Sea* (1866) and Jules Verne's *20,000 Leagues Under the Sea*

---

## Victorian Giant Squid Mania

### The Great Alecton Expedition (1861)
French corvette *Alecton* attempted to capture a giant squid near Madeira - estimated 50-60 feet long. Tried to haul it aboard but too heavy. Captain reportedly cut it free.

### Newfoundland Strandings (1870s)
- **1878:** Specimen washed ashore at Thimble Tickle Bay - mantle 6.1m (20 ft), tentacle 10.7m (35 ft), estimated weight 0.9 tonnes
- Moses Harvey (Reverend) famously kept one in his bathtub for study
- Local fishermen often processed specimens into manure or animal feed

### Scientific Recognition
- **Japetus Steenstrup** (1850s) first described *Architeuthis* genus
- **1857:** Name *Architeuthis dux* established
- By 1880s, giant squid gained scientific acceptance

## AKKOROKAMUI (アッコロカムイ)

**Culture:** Ainu (Indigenous people of Hokkaido, northern Japan)

**Etymology:** Ainu "At-kor-kamuy" = "string-holding kamuy (divine being)" - referring to tentacles

**Size:** 1 chō (approximately 1 hectare/100m+ across)

### Spider Origin Story:
Originally a gigantic spider named **Yaushikep** ("net-braiding-one") that ravaged the village of Rebunge. An elder prayed to sea god Repun Kamuy, who plunged the spider into Funka Bay, transforming it into the giant octopus.

### Undergarment Origin Story:
When goddess Kotan-kar-kamuy ascended to heaven, his wife cast her "under-belt" (8-cord braided garment called *pón-kut*) into the sea, which transformed into Akkorokamui.

### Appearance:
- **Entire body is red** - described as so intense the redness shines and reflects on sea and sky
- Detectable by the red glow from great distances
- Ships should steer clear
- Location: Uchiura Bay (Funka Bay), Iburi region, Hokkaido

### Rātoshikamui - The "Many-Tailed" Giant Octopus:
The mightiest sea creature, opposed by Huri Kamui (giant bird). They had a tug-of-war battle at the mouth of the Ishikari River:
- The octopus squirted ink
- Protruded its mouth with fierce eyes
- Huri bird spread wings, pecked at tentacles, tried to lift octopus
- Neither prevailed - resulted in stalemate
- During the battle, Huri's tail movements caused such turbulence that the sea became known as "Ishikari"

### Shinto Connections:
- Incorporated as minor kami (deity)
- Associated with **healing abilities** - ailments of limbs, skin, disfigurements, broken bones
- Characteristic: **self-amputation and regeneration** (like real octopuses)
- Offerings favored: fish, crab, mollusks

**Branding Phrases:**
- "From the depths of Hokkaido, a healer rises"
- "Regenerate. Adapt. Prevail."
- "When the sea turns red, Akkorokamui is watching"
- "The eight arms that hold everything together"

## KOROMODAKO (衣蛸) - Cloth Octopus

**Location:** Sea of Japan, particularly Kyoto and Fukui Prefectures (Ine and Wakasa Bays)

**Description:** Small octopuses; males a few centimeters, females up to 5x that length. When threatened, instantly grow massive enough to engulf ships whole.

**Name origin:** When enlarged, stretches like a cloth covering - can wrap around ships and drag them under

---

## TAKO NYŌBŌ (蛸女房) - Octopus Wife

**Origin:** Ishikawa Prefecture folklore

**Story:** Takes human woman form, marries human man, excellent housewife. Husband spies on wife transforming into octopus in washbasin. She uses her own body as soup stock - "washing her body and feeding husband her bathwater."

**Rule:** When true identity discovered, must return to sea forever

## TAKO NYŪDŌ (蛸入道) - Octopus Monk

**Location:** Sea of Japan, especially Shimane Prefecture

**Appearance:** Bulbous octopus head with bearded old man's face, 8 tentacles, wears human clothing like a bald priest

**Behavior:** Attacks fishermen, dragging them beneath waves

**Art:** Depicted in 1666 *Bakemono Emaki* by Kanō Munenobu

---

## FUNAYŪREI (船幽霊) - Ship Ghost

**Translation:** "ship ghost"

**Type:** Ghost of drowned sailors, vengeful spirit

**Appearance:** Ghost ships with spectral crew in white funerary robes; eerie luminescent mist

**Danger:** Attack ships by capsizing them, or dragging crews underwater

**Attack method:** Use buckets and ladles to flood ships with seawater

**Defense:** Carry buckets with holes; throw food/provisions as distraction

## LUSCA

**Culture:** Caribbean folklore (Bahamas, blue holes of Andros)

**Etymology:** Derived from "Mollusca"

**Description:** Giant octopus, giant cuttlefish, or half-shark, half-octopus hybrid. Size claims: Over 75 feet. Inhabits blue holes (underwater sinkholes).

**Blue Hole Facts:**
- Andros has 178 blue holes on land

**Key Narrative:**

**Kumugwe (Komokwa):** Undersea Chief, god of tides and maker of coppers. Bestows riches and supernatural treasures.

### Sedna (Nuliajuk/Sanna) - Inuit

Mistress of sea animals, created all living things. Often depicted with legs/feet transformed into fish tail. Known as "Sea Woman" or "Mother of the Sea."

## OCTOPUS INTELLIGENCE - BONCH

### B - Beauty
- Chromatophores allow skin to change color/texture in milliseconds
- Can display complex moving patterns across body
- Fluid, graceful movement with infinite degrees of freedom

### O - Intelligence
- **Three hearts**: Two branchial hearts pump blood to gills; systemic heart pumps to body. Blue blood (hemocyanin with copper)
- **500 million neurons**: More than dogs; comparable to some mammals
- **Distributed intelligence**: Two-thirds of neurons are in arms, not brain
- **Arm autonomy**: Severed arms continue responding for over an hour

### N - Nature
- Eight arms with hundreds of suction cups containing chemoreceptors
- Can **taste with arms**
- Soft body can squeeze through gaps as small as 2.5cm
- Parrot-like beak at center of arms

### C - Curiosity
- Tool use: Stack coconut shells for shelter
- Problem solving: Unscrew jar lids, open child-proof containers
- Escape artists: Climb out of tanks, enter other aquariums to feed
- Play behavior: Juggle objects, squirt water at lights

### Famous Octopus Facts
- Otto the octopus caused short circuits by spraying water at lamps
- Octopuses have been observed using Lego bricks to block their dens
- Each arm has "local knowledge" - can react independently even when severed

**Quotes:**
"Octopuses are 'the closest we will come to meeting an intelligent alien'" - Science of Us, 2016

## SQUID FACTS FOR ENGINEERS

### Jet Propulsion
- Draws water into mantle cavity, jets through siphon
- Speed: Up to 25 mph (40 km/h)
- Can jet in any direction by rotating siphon
- Gentle rhythmic pulses for cruising, explosive jet for escape

### Giant Squid Eyes
- Diameter: Up to 27cm (11 inches) - largest eyes of any living animal
- Pupil: 9cm across
- Detects bioluminescent light in deep darkness
- Only extinct ichthyosaurs had larger eyes

### Giant vs. Colossal Squid

| Feature | Giant Squid | Colossal Squid |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Scientific name | Architeuthis dux | Mesonychoteuthis hamiltoni |
| Maximum length | 13m (43 ft) | 4.2m (14 ft) mantle |
| Weight | Up to 275 kg | Up to 495 kg |
| Eyes | Up to 27cm | Up to 30-40cm |
| Arms | Suckers only | Hooks + suckers |
| Habitat | Worldwide | Antarctic |

### Colossal Squid Facts
- Heaviest invertebrate known
- Only squid in family with swiveling hooks on arms
- Lives in circumantarctic Southern Ocean
- Eyes glow: Photophores next to lens produce light
- Slow metabolism: Only needs ~30g of prey daily

## CEPHALOPOD INK - EVERYTHING

### Etymology
Greek "sēpia" = cuttlefish; describes brown ink color

### Chemistry
- **Main component**: Melanin (same pigment as human skin)
- **Other contents**: Mucus, tyrosinase, dopamine, L-DOPA
- **Amino acids**: Taurine, aspartic acid, glutamic acid, alanine, lysine
- **Color variations**: Octopuses = black, Squid = blue-black, Cuttlefish = brown

### Historical Uses
- **Writing ink**: Used for pens and quills throughout history
- **Art pigment**: Sepia ink created brown pigments for artists
- **Food coloring**: Still used in Mediterranean and Japanese cuisine
- **Photography**: Sepia toning for vintage photographs
- **Medicine**: Galen used it in compound drugs

### Behavioral Uses
- **Smoke screen**: Release dark cloud to obscure predator's view
- **Pseudomorphs**: "False bodies" that predators attack mistakenly
- **Blanch-ink-jet maneuver**: Color change + ink release + escape
- **Chemical alarm**: Ink functions as warning signal to other cephalopods

### Darwin Quote (Voyage of the Beagle)
"Watching the habits of an Octopus or cuttle-fish... they darted tail first, with the rapidity of an arrow... at the same instant discolouring the water with a dark chestnut-brown ink"
