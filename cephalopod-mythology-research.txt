## SCYLLA - The Six-Headed Sea Monster

**Source:** Homer, *Odyssey* Book XII (8th century BC); Ovid, *Metamorphoses*; Apollodorus

**Description:** "She has twelve feet all dangling down, six long necks with a grisly head on each of them, and in each head a triple row of crowded and close-set teeth" (Odyssey 12.85-95)

**Original myth:** A beautiful nymph transformed into a monster by Circe (jealous of Glaucus) or by Amphitrite

**Key Quotes:**
- "Better by far to lose six men and keep your ship than lose your entire crew."
- "No seaman ever, in any vessel, has boasted of sailing that way unharmed, for with every single head she snatches and carries off a man."
- "She has a girl's sweet face...below her midriff dogs, ringed in a raging row!"

**Symbolism:** Danger lurking just out of sight; forced choice between two evils (Scylla and Charybdis)

---

## PLINY THE ELDER - Giant Polypus

**Source:** *Naturalis Historia*, Book IX, Chapter 30 (1st century AD)

**Description:** A "monstrous polypus" that attacked ships and divers, drawing ships toward it with its arms, killing shipwrecked people. Called the "polypus of Carteia."

**Key Quote:** "The monstrous polypus...ripped apart shipwrecked people and divers."

---

## ARISTOTLE - Cephalopod Intelligence

**Source:** *History of Animals* (4th century BC)

**Key Facts:**
- First scientific observer to describe cephalopods systematically
- Described giant squid ("teuthus") up to 5 ell lengths (~7.5-10 feet)
- Noted ink defense mechanism
- Stated: "The octopus is the most intelligent of all invertebrates"

---

## CHARYBDIS - Whirlpool Monster

**Source:** Homer, *Odyssey* XII

**Description:** A whirlpool monster who swallowed and regurgitated the sea three times daily, located opposite Scylla in the Strait of Messina

## THE KRAKEN

**Etymology:** From Norwegian "kraken" - definite form of "krake" meaning "crooked creature" or "crookie" (from Old Norse "kraki" = hook)

**Christen Jensøn (1646):** First written description - sea monster with many arms that grabs boats and pulls them down into the abyss

**Olaus Magnus (1539-1555):** Carta marina showed sea monster between Norway and Iceland with tentacles growing from head. His 1555 *Historia de Gentibus Septentrionalibus* described:
- Black coloring
- Head 10-12 cubits long (~6-7 meters)
- Body 14-15 cubits (~8-9 meters)
- Eyes with 16-20 foot circumference, flaming red pupils
- "Beard" of hairs thick as goose feathers

**Erik Pontoppidan (1753):** Most influential description:
- "The monster is round, flat, and full of arms... the largest and most surprising of all the animal creation"
- Size: "quarter of a mile in circumference" when at surface
- Creates whirlpool/suction vortex when diving
- "If its arms laid hold of the largest man-of-war, they would pull it down to the bottom"

**Hans Egede (1729):** Described kraken's body as "many miles in length" with "many heads and a number of claws"

**Famous Tennyson Poem - "The Kraken" (1830):**
"Below the thunders of the upper deep,
Far, far beneath in the abysmal sea,
His ancient, dreamless, uninvaded sleep
The Kraken sleepeth..."

---

## HAFGUFA - Medieval Sea Monster

**Etymology:** Old Norse "haf" (sea) + "gufa" (steam/vapor) = "sea-reek"

**Source:** King's Mirror (Konungs skuggsjá, c. 1250)

**Description:** Appeared like land more than fish when surfacing. Only two exist. Opens mouth like "a great sound or fjord" - fish swim in, then it closes jaws and devours them all.

**Örvar-Odds saga:** Describes ship sailing BETWEEN the creature's jaws - the "rocks" were its nostrils and lower jaw

**2023 Discovery:** Whale "trap-feeding" behavior matches hafgufa description exactly - whales stay stationary with jaws open, appearing like islands

---

## SEA MONK (1546)

**Discovery:** Creature captured in Øresund strait (Denmark/Sweden) described as a "fish" resembling a human monk, 4 ells long (~15 feet). Sent to Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor.

**Japetus Steenstrup (1850s):** Suggested it was a giant squid - tentacles resembling monk's robes

---

## PIERRE DENYS DE MONTFORT (1801)

French malacologist who published famous engravings of "colossal octopus" attacking ships. Died "starving and poor in Paris around 1820" after his credibility was destroyed.

**Legacy:** Influenced Victor Hugo's *Toilers of the Sea* (1866) and Jules Verne's *20,000 Leagues Under the Sea*

## AKKOROKAMUI (アッコロカムイ)

**Culture:** Ainu (Indigenous people of Hokkaido, northern Japan)

**Etymology:** Ainu "At-kor-kamuy" = "string-holding kamuy (divine being)" - referring to tentacles

**Size:** 1 chō (approximately 1 hectare/100m+ across)

### Spider Origin Story:
Originally a gigantic spider named Yaushikep that ravaged the village of Rebunge. An elder prayed to sea god Repun Kamuy, who plunged the spider into Funka Bay, transforming it into the giant octopus.

### Undergarment Origin Story:
When goddess Kotan-kar-kamuy ascended to heaven, his wife cast her "under-belt" (8-cord braided garment) into the sea, which transformed into Akkorokamui.

### Appearance:
- **Entire body is red** - described as so intense the redness shines and reflects on sea and sky
- Detectable by the red glow from great distances
- Ships should steer clear

### Rātoshikamui - The "Many-Tailed" Giant Octopus:
The mightiest sea creature, opposed by Huri Kamui (giant bird). They had a tug-of-war battle at the mouth of the Ishikari River - the octopus squirted ink, Huri pecked at tentacles. Neither prevailed, resulting in stalemate.

### Shinto Connections:
- Incorporated as minor kami (deity)
- Associated with **healing abilities** - ailments of limbs, skin, disfigurements, broken bones
- Characteristic: **self-amputation and regeneration** (like real octopuses)
- Offerings favored: fish, crab, mollusks

**Key Phrases for Branding:**
- "From the depths of Hokkaido, a healer rises"
- "Regenerate. Adapt. Prevail."
- "When the sea turns red, Akkorokamui is watching"

## KOROMODAKO (衣蛸) - Cloth Octopus

**Location:** Sea of Japan (Kyoto, Fukui Prefectures)

**Description:** Small octopuses that when threatened, instantly grow massive enough to engulf ships whole. Name because when enlarged, stretches like cloth covering ships.

## LUSCA

**Culture:** Caribbean folklore (Bahamas, blue holes of Andros)

**Etymology:** Derived from "Mollusca"

**Description:** Giant octopus, giant cuttlefish, or half-shark, half-octopus hybrid. Size claims: Over 75 feet. Inhabits blue holes (underwater sinkholes).

**Key Narrative:**
- Attacks divers who venture too deep
- Creates dangerous whirlpools
- Emerges from underwater caves

**Pop Culture:**
- River Monsters TV show (Season 8, "Terror in Paradise") investigated
- Lusca Fantastic Film Fest in Puerto Rico
- Video game "Stranded Deep" features "Lusca the Great"

**Branding Phrases:**
- "75 feet of ancient hunger"
- "Born from Mollusca"
- "The thing from the blue holes"

### Nuu-chah-nulth - "Raven Annoys Octopus"

Full narrative: One morning, as tide went out, old people saw a woman walking along beach with long hair strung into **eight braids**. Her name was **Octopus**. Raven came along and "began to bother her."

### Kwakwaka'wakw

**Sisiutl:** Three-headed sea serpent whose glance turns adversaries to stone

**Kumugwe (Komokwa):** Undersea Chief, god of tides, bestows riches

## MAMI WATA - Water Spirit

**Culture:** West African with strong Caribbean diaspora connections

**Description:** Beautiful light-skinned woman with very long, light-colored hair. Sometimes lower half fish/mermaid. Lives in mansion under water. Grants wealth in exchange for devotion.

**Caribbean Connections:** Lasirèn (Haiti), Mae d'Agua, Maman de l'Eau

## OCTOPUS INTELLIGENCE - BONCH

### B - Beauty
- Chromatophores allow skin to change color/texture in milliseconds
- Can display complex moving patterns across body

### O - Intelligence
- **Three hearts** (two branchial, one systemic)
- **Blue blood** (hemocyanin with copper)
- **500 million neurons** (more than dogs)
- **Distributed intelligence**: Two-thirds of neurons in arms, not brain
- **Arm autonomy**: Severed arms continue responding for over an hour

### N - Nature
- Eight arms with hundreds of suction cups containing chemoreceptors
- Can **taste with arms**
- Soft body can squeeze through gaps as small as 2.5cm

### C - Curiosity
- Tool use: Stack coconut shells for shelter
- Problem solving: Unscrew jar lids, open child-proof containers
- Escape artists: Climb out of tanks, enter other aquariums to feed
- Play behavior: Juggle objects

**Famous Octopus Facts:**
- Otto the octopus caused short circuits by spraying water at lamps
- Each arm has "local knowledge" - can react independently even when severed

**Quotes:**
- "Octopuses are 'the closest we will come to meeting an intelligent alien'" - Science of Us, 2016

## SQUID FACTS FOR ENGINEERS

### Jet Propulsion
- Draws water into mantle cavity, jets through siphon
- Speed: Up to 25 mph (40 km/h)
- Can jet in any direction by rotating siphon

### Giant Squid Eyes
- Diameter: Up to 27cm (11 inches) - largest eyes of any living animal
- Pupil: 9cm across
- Detects bioluminescent light in deep darkness

### Giant vs. Colossal Squid

| Feature | Giant Squid | Colossal Squid |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Scientific name | Architeuthis dux | Mesonychoteuthis hamiltoni |
| Maximum length | 13m (43 ft) | 4.2m (14 ft) mantle |
| Weight | Up to 275 kg | Up to 495 kg |
| Eyes | Up to 27cm | Up to 30-40cm |

## CEPHALOPOD INK & "SEPIA"

**Etymology:** Greek "sēpia" = cuttlefish; describes brown ink color

**Chemistry:** Melanin-based with mucus, tyrosinase, dopamine, L-DOPA

**Historical Uses:**
- Writing ink for pens/quills
- Art pigment for artists
- Food coloring in Mediterranean/Japanese cuisine

**Behavioral Uses:**
- Smoke screen to obscure predator's view
- Pseudomorphs ("false bodies" predators attack)
- Chemical alarm signal to other cephalopods

**Darwin Quote (Voyage of the Beagle):**
"Watching the habits of an Octopus or cuttle-fish... they darted tail first, with the rapidity of an arrow... at the same instant discolouring the water with a dark chestnut-brown ink"
